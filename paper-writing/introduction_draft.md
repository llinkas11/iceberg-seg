# Introduction

Iceberg calving is a major component of Greenland Ice Sheet mass loss (Enderlin et al., 2014; Alley et al., 2023). Accurate iceberg area inventories are required to estimate submarine melt-driven freshwater fluxes into fjord systems (Moon et al., 2018; Rezvanbehbahani et al., 2020) and to parameterize iceberg drift and deterioration models used in maritime hazard forecasting (Keghouche et al., 2010; Marchenko et al., 2019). Satellite optical imagery, in particular the Sentinel-2 near-infrared band (B08) at 10 m resolution, is the primary tool for retrieving above-waterline iceberg areas in Greenland fjords (Sulak et al., 2017; Scheick et al., 2019). Icebergs smaller than 100 m in root length contribute disproportionately to the total freshwater budget but are systematically underrepresented in existing inventories because they fall near or below the detection limits of per-pixel methods (Rezvanbehbahani et al., 2020). Extending reliable retrieval across all iceberg size classes and across the full range of seasonal illumination conditions is therefore necessary for closing regional calving budgets (Moyer et al., 2019).

The standard approach to optical iceberg detection applies a fixed reflectance threshold to B08, classifying pixels above a calibrated cutoff as ice (Sulak et al., 2017; Moyer et al., 2019; Scheick et al., 2019). Fisser et al. (2024) calibrated a threshold of B08 >= 0.12 against airborne reference data at SZA 56 degrees and evaluated its stability across 14 Kangerlussuaq acquisitions spanning SZA 45 to 81 degrees. Below SZA 65 degrees, standardized area error remained within +5.9% to -5.7%; above 65 degrees, error grew and became inconsistent. Two physical mechanisms drive this degradation. At high SZA, iceberg surfaces segregate into sun-facing slopes and shadowed faces, causing shadow pixels to fall below the threshold and producing systematic area underestimation (Fisser et al., 2024; Gardner and Sharp, 2010). Simultaneously, ocean NIR reflectance rises, compressing the spectral contrast between ice and water and inflating false-positive rates (Pegau and Paulson, 2001). Fisser et al. (2024) characterized these errors under manually curated conditions: cloud cover below 2%, sea ice concentration below approximately 10%, and only icebergs with root length above 100 m. The European Space Agency recommends a SZA limit of 70 degrees for Sentinel-2 acquisitions (ESA, 2019). What is needed, but does not yet exist, is a retrieval approach that remains robust to the dual failure modes of shadowing and ocean brightening at SZA well above 65 degrees, that operates under automated rather than manually curated processing conditions, and that does not exclude the small-iceberg population from its detection scope.

Convolutional neural networks encode spatial relationships between neighboring pixels, offering a mechanism for distinguishing shadowed ice from open water that per-pixel thresholds lack. Encoder-decoder architectures have demonstrated strong segmentation performance in cryosphere remote sensing, including iceberg delineation in Greenland fjords (Rezvanbehbahani et al., 2020) and calving front extraction from SAR imagery (Baumhoer et al., 2019). This study trains a UNet++ model (Zhou et al., 2018) with a ResNet34 encoder (He et al., 2016) on 984 labeled Sentinel-2 chips spanning low-SZA conditions from Fisser et al. (2025) and high-SZA conditions annotated for this work, and compares its area retrieval against fixed and adaptive NIR threshold baselines across 175 scenes in Kangerlussuaq and Sermilik fjords, east Greenland. Scenes are stratified into four SZA bins from below 65 degrees to above 75 degrees. Unlike Fisser et al. (2024), no minimum iceberg size is imposed and chip selection is automated using scene-classification and sea-ice filters rather than manual scene curation, better approximating operational monitoring conditions. Three questions structure the analysis:

1. To what extent does UNet++ segmentation, which incorporates learned spatial context, reduce the SZA-dependent area bias that fixed and adaptive NIR thresholding exhibits above 65 degrees?
2. At extreme SZA (above 70 degrees, beyond ESA's recommended acquisition limit), how does the variance and magnitude of UNet++ area retrieval compare to threshold baselines across two independent fjord systems?
3. Does applying adaptive thresholding to UNet++ probability surfaces yield more accurate area estimates than applying either learned segmentation or reflectance thresholding independently, and to what extent does morphological top-hat filtering improve recovery of the sub-100 m iceberg population across illumination bins?

This paper demonstrates that learned segmentation maintains area retrieval consistency at solar zenith angles where threshold methods produce order-of-magnitude overestimates, evaluates hybrid pipeline configurations that combine learned and threshold-based approaches, and quantifies the contribution of morphological post-processing to small-iceberg recovery. Section 2 describes the study design, imagery, and detection methods. Section 3 presents area retrieval results stratified by SZA bin and fjord. Section 4 discusses the implications for extending automated iceberg monitoring into the low-illumination season.
